Here is the complete architectural breakdown of the machine learning pipeline we just built.

Building a model for high-dimensional, low-sample-size (HDLSS) biological data is vastly different from standard tabular data. Here is exactly what we did, stage by stage, and the mathematical and clinical reasoning behind every decision.

Phase 1: The Data Foundation
Before any machine learning could happen, we had to structurally secure the dataset. The core challenge was 1,149 features against only 945 patients, which is a recipe for catastrophic overfitting.

Targeted Imputation & Cleaning: We dropped irrelevant columns (no., eeg.date) and an empty artifact (Unnamed: 122). We had missing values in IQ and education.

Why: Instead of using a global average, we imputed these using the median of each specific main.disorder group. This prevented us from blurring the clinical lines between a healthy control and a severe cognitive disorder.

Stratified 3-Way Split (Train/Val/Test): We locked away 15% of the data as a pure Test set, and split the rest into Training (70%) and Validation (15%). We used strict stratification.

Why: Stratification ensures the exact same ratio of rare disorders (like OCD) exists across all sets. Locking the Test set early prevents "Data Leakage"—where the model accidentally peeks at the final exam before studying.

Isolated Scaling: We applied StandardScaler to normalize the brainwave amplitudes, but we fit the scaler entirely on the Training set, and only transformed the Validation and Test sets.

Why: If you scale the whole dataset at once, the mathematical average of the test set leaks into the training set, giving you artificially high (fake) accuracy.

Phase 2: The Binary Screener (Healthy vs. Patient)
In clinical settings, doctors first determine if a pathology exists at all before sub-typing it. We transformed the 7-class problem into a binary classification problem: Healthy (0) vs. Patient (1).

The Baseline Network: We built a Multi-Layer Perceptron (Neural Network) with heavy L2 Regularization (Dropout/Weight penalties). It hit ~90% accuracy, but failed completely at identifying Healthy Controls (only 21% recall).

Why: The data was heavily imbalanced (594 Patients vs. 67 Healthy Controls). The model realized it could just guess "Patient" every time and look smart.

The SMOTE Failure & Threshold Solution: We tried using SMOTE to mathematically generate "fake" healthy EEG data to balance the scales. It failed.

Why: SMOTE interpolates data mathematically, which created biologically impossible brainwaves that confused the model. Instead, we abandoned fake data and Tuned the Probability Threshold. We told the model: "If you are even 15% confident this person is Healthy, label them Healthy." This successfully restored our ability to catch healthy brains without compromising patient data.

Phase 3: Dimensionality Reduction & Feature Engineering
Once a patient was flagged as "ill," we needed to figure out which of the 6 disorders they had. Feeding the network all 1,144 features caused severe feature dilution (the model blanked out on harder classes like Schizophrenia and OCD). We had to compress the noise and amplify the biological signal.

Engineering Clinical Biomarkers: We calculated the average Absolute Power across the Frontal Lobe and created ratios (Theta-to-Beta, Theta-to-Alpha).

Why: Machine learning models struggle to divide columns against each other dynamically. By explicitly handing the model these ratios, we provided known clinical biomarkers for cognitive strain and attentional deficits.

Network Compression (PCA on Coherence): The dataset contained 1,026 Coherence (COH) features. Coherence measures how different brain regions sync up. Because everything is connected, these 1,026 columns were heavily correlated and mostly noise. We used Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to crush those 1,026 features down into 15 dense "Network Connectivity Components."

Why: This reduced our feature space from an unmanageable 1,149 columns down to a highly concentrated 133 columns, starving the model of noise and forcing it to look at structural reality.

Phase 4: The Differential Diagnosis Engine
For the final multi-class (6-way) diagnosis, Neural Networks were still too prone to overfitting on this small sample size. We pivoted to a Tree-Based Ensemble.

LightGBM (Gradient Boosting): We deployed a LightGBM classifier on the compressed 133-feature dataset. We kept the trees shallow (max_depth=5) and forced it to use only 80% of the data and 80% of the features per tree.

Why: Gradient Boosters build trees sequentially, with each tree trying to fix the errors of the last one. They are naturally immune to differing feature scales (raw ratios vs. PCA components) and handle extreme multi-class imbalances beautifully when class_weight='balanced' is applied.

The Final Result
By structuring the architecture this way, your model went from predicting pure noise (0% on minority classes) to a hidden test accuracy of ~58% across 6 complex psychiatric conditions (with Random Guessing being ~16%).

Most importantly, the architecture successfully learned the biological signatures of the hardest conditions, yielding massive real-world F1-Scores for OCD (80%), Mood Disorders (73%), and Schizophrenia (68%).
